The two usual suspects were already waiting by the door.

I’m no expert, but nobody will convince me that my cats don’t associate the sound of my espresso machine with my office door opening. It was 3:45 in the morning. They should have been asleep—or doing whatever cats do at that hour—but there they were, sitting shoulder to shoulder as if they’d been waiting for me.

I failed, once again, to sneak into my office without them noticing.

Gilberto looked up first. Medroso followed half a second later. Both stared at me with the quiet confidence of creatures that already knew how the next few minutes would unfold.

I smiled, stepped around them, and closed the door.

For another moment they stayed outside, little heads tilted, stretching just enough to see through the narrowing gap before the latch clicked shut.

My office welcomed me back exactly as I’d left it a few hours earlier.

The terminal still displayed the latest version of the diagram I’d been reorganizing the night before. Or maybe it was only two hours ago. Hard to tell. Three browser windows were waiting where I’d left them. One showed the diagram rendered in the browser. Another was paused on a login screen. The third held the presentation I would be walking through later that morning.

Cookie shifted softly in the enclosure by the window, already awake, already pacing with quiet purpose.

I started where I always started.

The second paragraph.

One sentence bothered me before I finished reading it.

It wasn’t wrong.

It simply answered a question nobody had asked yet.

I deleted it.

Better.

Farther down, a diagram that had collected another layer of arrows during the week suddenly looked heavier than I remembered. Every relationship was technically correct. Together, they asked the audience to carry more than they needed to.

Half of it disappeared.

There you are.

The explanation finally read the way I had intended it to.

The house was still asleep. The only sounds were the occasional scratch at the office door, the soft rustle from Cookie’s enclosure, and the familiar rhythm of my mechanical keyboard.

By the time my coffee was cold, the scratching had stopped.

The next slide made the same point twice.

Different words.

Different diagram.

Same destination.

I merged them into one, rewrote the title, read it aloud, rewrote it again, then kept moving.

One section naturally opened into a larger conversation.

I followed it for a few sentences.

It was interesting.

Maybe too interesting.

I stopped.

That wasn’t today’s conversation.

I closed the section where it already did the job I needed it to do and moved on.

By the time the sun finally began finding its way through the blinds, the presentation felt lighter.

Not smaller.

Just easier to walk through.

I saved the file, closed my laptop halfway, and walked toward the kitchen to refill my coffee, leaving the office door slightly open behind me.

When I came back, every cat in the house was gathered just outside the office.

None of them even looked at me.

All four were sitting in a perfect line, eyes fixed past the doorway, completely absorbed by Cookie’s enclosure and the tiny green lizards darting along the window outside.

I laughed.

For weeks I had assumed they wanted into my office.

They never did.

My office was simply the door that happened to lead to the thing they actually cared about.

I stood there longer than I expected.

Then I looked back toward the presentation waiting on my desk.

For years I had been polishing diagrams, tightening explanations, removing every opportunity for someone to get lost.

None of that work was wasted.

But somewhere along the way I had started treating the presentation as the destination instead of the doorway.

The presentation was never the point.

It was only the door.
